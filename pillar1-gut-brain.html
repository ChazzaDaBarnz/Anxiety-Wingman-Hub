1
Foundation
What Is the Gut-Brain Axis?

The gut-brain axis is the two-way communication highway running between your gastrointestinal tract and your central nervous system. It's not metaphor — it's a real, measurable, bidirectional network of neural, hormonal, and immune signals constantly flowing between your digestive system and your brain.

Think of it as a live phone line. Both parties are talking at the same time, constantly. Your brain affects gut motility, secretion, and immune function. Your gut affects mood, cognitive function, stress response, and yes — the panic response.

The key insight: Most people think of anxiety as a brain problem. Emerging science increasingly treats it as a whole-body problem — and the gut is a primary player, not a passive bystander.

Three Communication Pathways

  • Neural pathway: The vagus nerve (direct neural line between gut and brain), plus the enteric nervous system embedded in the gut wall itself
  • Hormonal/endocrine pathway: Gut cells produce hormones like serotonin, ghrelin, and GLP-1 that reach the brain through the bloodstream
  • Immune/inflammatory pathway: Gut bacteria influence immune cells, which produce cytokines that cross into the brain and alter mood and cognition

How Scientists Proved It Goes Both Ways

For decades, the assumption was simple: the brain controls the gut. Stress causes stomach problems. That part is true. But the research that changed everything came from germ-free animal studies — mice raised with no gut bacteria at all.

These germ-free mice showed drastically abnormal stress responses. When researchers colonized them with bacteria from anxious mice, the germ-free mice became anxious. When they colonized them with bacteria from calm mice, they became calm. The gut microbiome was actively shaping behavior — not just responding to it.

In humans, studies have shown that people with irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) have significantly higher rates of anxiety and depression. Not because anxiety causes IBS — but because both conditions often share the same root: a dysregulated gut-brain axis. Fix the gut, and mood often improves. Fix the anxiety, and gut symptoms often improve. The causation runs both directions simultaneously.

  • The HPA (hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal) axis — your stress response system — is heavily influenced by gut microbiota composition
  • Gut permeability (leaky gut) increases during psychological stress, allowing bacterial byproducts to enter circulation and trigger systemic inflammation
  • Fecal transplants from humans with depression to germ-free rodents have produced depressive behavior in the animals — the microbiome transferred the mood state

Anatomy
Your Second Brain: The Enteric Nervous System

The enteric nervous system (ENS) is a network of 100 to 500 million neurons embedded in the walls of your gastrointestinal tract — from esophagus to rectum. Scientists call it the "second brain" for a reason: it contains more neurons than your spinal cord, and it can operate completely independently of your brain.

This isn't a support system for the brain. It's a peer. The ENS runs digestion, regulates gut immune response, and manages local reflex arcs — all without waiting for instruction from above.

500M+

neurons in the enteric nervous system — more than the entire spinal cord. The gut doesn't wait for orders from your brain.

What the ENS Actually Does

  • Coordinates peristalsis (the muscular contractions that move food through the digestive tract)
  • Regulates enzyme secretion, blood flow to gut tissue, and local immune responses
  • Detects nutrients, toxins, and mechanical stretch in the gut wall
  • Uses the same neurotransmitters as the brain — serotonin, dopamine, acetylcholine, norepinephrine, GABA
  • Survives and functions even after the vagus nerve is severed — truly independent operation
Why this matters for panic disorder: If your gut's nervous system is dysregulated — because of poor diet, dysbiosis, or chronic inflammation — it sends distress signals upward. Not metaphorical distress. Actual neural signals that the brain interprets as threat, amplifying the panic response.

The ENS Forms Before the Brain — and Shares Its Origins

During fetal development, the enteric nervous system and the central nervous system arise from the same embryonic tissue — the neural crest cells. This shared origin means the two systems are not just connected; they're fundamentally related. Disruptions to gut development can have downstream effects on central nervous system function, and vice versa.

For people with panic disorder, this shared origin matters. Research has consistently found higher rates of functional GI disorders — IBS, functional dyspepsia, GERD — among people with anxiety and panic disorder compared to the general population. This isn't coincidence. It's a signal that both the ENS and CNS are running dysregulated, often for the same underlying reasons: chronic stress, dysbiosis, inflammation.

The practical implication: if you're trying to support someone with panic disorder and you're only addressing the brain end of this system — therapy, medication — you're potentially leaving the ENS in a chronic state of distress that keeps feeding signals upward into an already sensitized central nervous system. The gut is part of the treatment picture.

Neural Pathway
The Vagus Nerve Highway

The vagus nerve is the longest cranial nerve in your body — it wanders from your brainstem all the way down through your heart, lungs, and digestive tract. It's the primary physical highway of the gut-brain axis.

Here's what most people get wrong: they assume the brain is sending instructions down to the gut. In reality, approximately 80% of vagal nerve fibers carry information from the gut to the brain — not the other way around. Your gut is doing most of the talking.

80% gut → brain. The vagus nerve is primarily an upward communication line. What's happening in your gut is constantly being reported to your brain — which means gut dysfunction directly shapes how your brain perceives threat, stress, and safety.

What the Vagus Nerve Regulates

  • Heart rate variability (HRV) — the flexibility of the heart's response to stress
  • The parasympathetic "rest and digest" response (the opposite of fight-or-flight)
  • Gut motility, enzyme secretion, and gut immune function
  • Inflammatory regulation — the vagus nerve actively suppresses inflammation via the cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway
  • Communication of satiety, nausea, and gut distress signals to the brain

Vagal Tone: The Missing Piece

Vagal tone refers to how active and responsive your vagus nerve is. High vagal tone means the nerve is functioning well — your body shifts smoothly between stress and recovery. Low vagal tone means the parasympathetic brake is weak, and your nervous system gets stuck in high-alert mode longer than it should.

People with panic disorder consistently show lower vagal tone and lower heart rate variability. This isn't a permanent condition — vagal tone is trainable. Diet, breathing, exercise, and environment all influence it directly.

The Gut-Vagus-Brain Loop You Can Influence

The vagus nerve doesn't just passively transmit gut signals — it actively monitors the gut environment. Short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), produced when gut bacteria ferment dietary fiber, directly stimulate vagal afferent neurons in the gut wall. Higher fiber intake → more SCFA production → stronger vagal signaling → better parasympathetic tone.

Polyphenols — found in berries, green tea, dark chocolate, and extra virgin olive oil — also influence vagal activity by supporting microbiome diversity and reducing gut inflammation, both of which the vagus nerve monitors and reports upward.

Practically: a gut full of fermented foods, diverse fiber, and polyphenol-rich plants is sending better signals up the vagus nerve, and a better-fed vagus nerve supports the kind of parasympathetic regulation that helps dampen panic responses. This is Pillar I applied: you're not just eating for energy — you're eating to train your nervous system's recovery capacity.

  • High-fiber foods (aim for 30+ grams/day): legumes, oats, vegetables, fruit with skin
  • Fermented foods: kimchi, sauerkraut, kefir, plain yogurt with live cultures, kombucha
  • Polyphenol sources: blueberries, dark chocolate (>70%), green tea, extra virgin olive oil, coffee (in moderation)
  • Omega-3 fatty acids: fatty fish, walnuts, flaxseed — reduce gut inflammation, support vagal function

Chemistry
Neurotransmitters Made in Your Gut

When most people think about serotonin and dopamine, they picture the brain. But the truth is your gut is a major neurochemical manufacturing facility — and what it produces affects your mood, anxiety levels, and panic response in ways that no amount of cognitive reframing can fully compensate for if the factory is broken.

90–95%

of your body's serotonin is produced in the gut — not the brain. The gut is the primary serotonin manufacturer.

Key Neurotransmitters Produced or Influenced in the Gut

Serotonin

Enterochromaffin cells lining the gut wall produce the vast majority of the body's serotonin. Gut serotonin regulates intestinal movement, bowel function, and sends signals up the vagus nerve. Gut bacteria directly influence how much serotonin these cells produce — dysbiosis reduces serotonin synthesis. Low serotonin is strongly associated with anxiety, depression, and heightened panic response.

GABA (Gamma-Aminobutyric Acid)

GABA is your brain's primary inhibitory neurotransmitter — the neurochemical brake that calms an overactive nervous system. Certain gut bacteria, particularly Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium strains, produce GABA directly. Animal studies show that specific probiotic strains reduce anxiety behavior — and that this effect is eliminated when the vagus nerve is cut, confirming the gut-to-brain pathway. Most anti-anxiety medications (benzodiazepines) work by enhancing GABA activity. Your gut bacteria can do this naturally — if the right bacteria are present.

Dopamine

Approximately 50% of the body's dopamine is produced in the gut. Gut dopamine regulates gut motility and is involved in reward signaling pathways that extend into the brain. Dysbiosis-related reductions in gut dopamine production have been linked to reduced motivation, anhedonia, and worsened mood states.

Norepinephrine

Some gut bacteria stimulate the production of norepinephrine in the gut. Norepinephrine is the primary catecholamine behind the fight-or-flight response. An overproduction state — which dysbiosis can contribute to — maintains a baseline of physiological alertness that makes panic attacks both more likely and harder to recover from.

The Paradox of Gut Serotonin and SSRIs

SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors) are the most commonly prescribed medications for anxiety and panic disorder. They work by preventing serotonin from being reabsorbed too quickly, keeping more of it available in the brain's synaptic gaps. This is often effective — but there's a dimension that's rarely discussed with patients.

Because 90-95% of serotonin is produced in the gut, the gut's serotonin environment significantly affects the total serotonin pool available to the system. If the gut microbiome is producing insufficient serotonin precursors, if gut inflammation is dysregulating enterochromaffin cells, or if dietary tryptophan intake is inadequate — no amount of reuptake inhibition makes up for insufficient production. You can't recycle what isn't being made.

This is partly why SSRIs work well for some people and not others, and why adding dietary intervention to pharmaceutical treatment often produces better outcomes than either approach alone. The gut end of the serotonin equation matters.

  • Tryptophan (serotonin precursor) found in: turkey, chicken, eggs, dairy, nuts, seeds, oats
  • Tryptophan needs adequate B6, B12, folate, and zinc to convert to serotonin — micronutrient deficiencies block the pathway
  • Gut inflammation reduces tryptophan conversion to serotonin and redirects it toward kynurenine pathway (associated with depression)

Ecosystem
The Microbiome & Mental Health

You are home to approximately 38 trillion microbial cells — bacteria, fungi, archaea, viruses — most of them living in your large intestine. This ecosystem, collectively called the gut microbiome, weighs roughly 2–3 pounds and contains over 1,000 distinct species. It is one of the most complex biological systems we've ever tried to understand.

What we know is enough to fundamentally change how we approach mental health. The microbiome directly influences brain chemistry, immune function, inflammation, hormone regulation, and — critically — the stress and panic response.

What Dysbiosis Looks Like

Dysbiosis is a disruption in the normal balance of gut microbial communities. It doesn't mean "bad bacteria" took over. It means the ratio and diversity shifted — often fewer beneficial species, less overall diversity, and sometimes an overgrowth of opportunistic organisms. Dysbiosis can develop from:

  • Antibiotic use (kills beneficial bacteria along with pathogens)
  • Chronic stress (stress hormones alter gut motility and microbial environment)
  • Ultra-processed food diets (starves beneficial bacteria, feeds harmful ones)
  • Insufficient dietary fiber (beneficial bacteria eat fiber — remove it and they decline)
  • Inadequate sleep (microbiome has its own circadian rhythm)
  • Chronic alcohol use
  • C-section birth and formula feeding (affects early microbiome establishment)
The diversity principle: Higher microbial diversity consistently correlates with better mental health outcomes. A monoculture gut — dominated by only a few species — is a vulnerable gut. The goal is not specific "good bacteria" so much as a rich, diverse ecosystem.

Psychobiotics: Bacteria That Change Your Mind

The term "psychobiotic" was coined by researchers John Cryan and Ted Dinan to describe probiotics that produce measurable mental health benefits. Certain strains of Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium have been shown in clinical trials to reduce anxiety, lower cortisol, and improve mood — through direct production of neurotransmitter precursors, SCFA production, and immune modulation.

Specific Bacteria Linked to Anxiety and Panic

Lactobacillus rhamnosus: One of the most studied strains for anxiety. In rodent models, significantly reduces anxiety-like behavior, reduces stress-induced corticosterone, and increases GABA receptor expression in the brain — effects abolished by vagotomy (cutting the vagus nerve), confirming the gut-brain pathway. Human trials have shown reduced cortisol and anxiety scores with supplementation.

Bifidobacterium longum: Shown to reduce anxiety-like behavior, lower stress response, and improve cognitive performance under stress in both animal and human studies. Influences the HPA axis (stress response system) directly.

Lactobacillus helveticus + Bifidobacterium longum combination: A randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial showed this combination significantly reduced anxiety and depression scores, reduced urinary cortisol levels, and reduced the neurotic dimension of personality in healthy volunteers.

Practical note: strains matter, but a diverse microbiome built on fermented foods and high fiber provides a broader base than any single probiotic supplement. Supplements are useful for acute restoration; diet is the long game.

Root Cause
Inflammation: The Hidden Link to Anxiety

Inflammation is the body's immune response — a necessary defense against infection, injury, and foreign substances. Acute inflammation heals you. Chronic low-grade inflammation, running quietly in the background for months or years, is a different animal entirely.

Chronic systemic inflammation is now strongly linked to anxiety disorders, depression, and cognitive impairment. The mechanism runs through the gut: gut dysbiosis and increased intestinal permeability allow bacterial byproducts to enter the bloodstream, triggering a systemic inflammatory response that eventually reaches the brain.

Neuroinflammation and the Panic Response

When inflammatory cytokines (signaling proteins like IL-6, TNF-α, and IL-1β) cross the blood-brain barrier or activate brain immune cells (microglia), they directly alter the brain's chemistry and function:

  • Reduced serotonin synthesis and availability
  • Increased tryptophan shunting toward the kynurenine pathway (produces compounds linked to depression and neurotoxicity rather than serotonin)
  • Heightened amygdala reactivity — the brain's threat-detection center becomes oversensitive
  • Impaired prefrontal cortex function — reducing the capacity to regulate emotional responses
  • Dysregulated HPA axis — the stress system runs hotter and recovers more slowly
The inflammation-panic loop: Chronic inflammation raises the baseline threat sensitivity of the brain. A sensitized brain generates more frequent and more intense panic responses to neutral stimuli. Panic attacks trigger stress hormones. Stress hormones worsen gut permeability and dysbiosis. Dysbiosis drives more inflammation. The loop closes.

The Mediterranean Diet and Mental Health Evidence

The Mediterranean dietary pattern — olive oil, fish, vegetables, legumes, nuts, seeds, moderate whole grains, minimal processed food — is the most evidence-backed anti-inflammatory diet pattern for mental health. The SMILES trial (2017) showed that dietary intervention producing a Mediterranean-style eating pattern led to significantly greater reductions in depression scores compared to social support alone, with 32% of dietary group achieving remission versus 8% in control group.

For anxiety specifically, a 2019 meta-analysis found that dietary interventions significantly reduced anxiety symptoms, with the strongest effects from interventions that increased fruit and vegetable intake, reduced processed food consumption, and incorporated healthy fats.

Key anti-inflammatory foods with evidence behind them:

  • Fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel): Omega-3s EPA and DHA directly reduce inflammatory cytokine production and have been shown to reduce anxiety in clinical trials
  • Extra virgin olive oil: Oleocanthal has NSAID-like anti-inflammatory properties; polyphenols support microbiome diversity
  • Turmeric/curcumin: Potent anti-inflammatory, crosses blood-brain barrier, shown to reduce anxiety and depression-like behavior; bioavailability increased dramatically with black pepper (piperine)
  • Leafy greens: Magnesium, folate, and nitrates reduce inflammatory markers; magnesium deficiency is linked to increased anxiety and heightened stress response
  • Berries: High polyphenol content, particularly anthocyanins, reduces neuroinflammation and improves cognitive function

Barrier Function
Leaky Gut & Panic Disorder

"Leaky gut" — technically called increased intestinal permeability — was once dismissed as fringe medicine. It's now a legitimate and active area of gastroenterology and immunology research. The basic concept is simple: your gut lining is supposed to be selectively permeable, allowing nutrients in and keeping harmful substances out. When the tight junctions between gut cells break down, things get in that shouldn't.

What gets through includes bacterial lipopolysaccharides (LPS) — fragments of bacterial cell walls that are potent triggers of systemic immune activation. Once in the bloodstream, LPS drives the inflammatory cascade that, as we've seen, has direct effects on brain chemistry and the anxiety response.

What Damages the Gut Barrier

  • Chronic psychological stress: Cortisol and corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) directly increase intestinal permeability — the stress-gut connection is measurable and direct
  • Ultra-processed foods: Emulsifiers (polysorbate-80, carboxymethylcellulose) common in processed foods have been shown to disrupt the mucus layer protecting the gut barrier
  • Alcohol: Directly damages tight junctions and gut epithelial cells
  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen, aspirin): Long-term use significantly increases gut permeability
  • Dysbiosis: Loss of beneficial bacteria that produce short-chain fatty acids needed to maintain gut barrier integrity
  • Gluten: In susceptible individuals, triggers zonulin release — a protein that opens tight junctions
The stress-permeability loop: Panic disorder creates chronic stress. Chronic stress damages the gut barrier. A leaky gut drives systemic inflammation. Systemic inflammation sensitizes the brain's threat detection. A more sensitive threat detection system makes panic attacks more frequent and more intense. The gut is both a victim and an amplifier of the disorder.

Evidence-Based Approaches to Restoring Gut Barrier Integrity

L-Glutamine: The primary fuel source for enterocytes (gut lining cells). Supplementation has shown ability to restore tight junction proteins and reduce permeability. Typically dosed at 5-10g daily in clinical studies. Found naturally in protein-rich foods, especially red meat, eggs, and dairy.

Zinc: Essential for tight junction integrity. Zinc deficiency is common in people who eat ultra-processed diets and is associated with increased gut permeability. Zinc carnosine specifically has been studied for gut barrier repair.

Bone broth: Contains gelatin, collagen peptides, glycine, proline, and glutamine — all precursors for gut lining repair. Traditional across virtually every culture for a reason.

Short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs): Produced by gut bacteria fermenting dietary fiber. Butyrate specifically is the primary energy source for colonocytes (colon lining cells) and is critical for maintaining barrier function. You produce more butyrate with more diverse, high-fiber diet.

Fermented foods: Beyond probiotics, the organic acids produced during fermentation (lactic acid, acetic acid) help restore the acid environment beneficial bacteria prefer and reduce pathogen colonization opportunity.

Timeline: Significant gut barrier improvement typically requires 4-12 weeks of consistent dietary intervention. This is not a quick fix — it's a sustained protocol.

Practical
Foods That Heal vs. Foods That Harm

This isn't about clean eating ideology. This is about what the research shows specifically affects gut health, the microbiome, intestinal barrier function, and neuroinflammation — all of which feed directly into the anxiety and panic cycle.

✓ Foods That Support the System
  • Fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel, herring)
  • Fermented foods: kimchi, sauerkraut, kefir, plain yogurt, miso, tempeh
  • High-fiber vegetables: broccoli, artichokes, asparagus, onions, garlic
  • Legumes: lentils, chickpeas, black beans
  • Whole grains: oats, quinoa, barley
  • Berries: blueberries, raspberries, blackberries
  • Leafy greens: spinach, kale, Swiss chard
  • Extra virgin olive oil
  • Walnuts and flaxseed (omega-3)
  • Dark chocolate (>70% cacao)
  • Green tea and chamomile
  • Bone broth
  • Turmeric with black pepper
  • Ginger
✗ Foods That Disrupt the System
  • Ultra-processed foods (anything with long ingredient lists)
  • Added sugar and high-fructose corn syrup
  • Artificial sweeteners (disrupt microbiome composition)
  • Refined carbohydrates: white bread, pastries, crackers
  • Industrial seed oils: soybean, corn, canola, sunflower (high omega-6)
  • Alcohol (damages gut barrier directly)
  • Food dyes and artificial preservatives
  • Emulsifiers in processed foods (disrupt gut mucus layer)
  • Excessive caffeine (increases cortisol, can worsen anxiety)
  • Gluten (for sensitive individuals)
  • Processed meats with nitrates
  • Fast food (combines multiple disruptors)
The 80/20 principle: This isn't about perfection. It's about shifting the ratio over time. Even moving from a primarily processed food diet to one with more whole foods, fermented foods, and dietary fiber makes measurable microbiome changes within 2-3 weeks. Progress beats perfection every time.

The Three Dietary Inputs That Most Directly Spike Panic

Caffeine: A potent adenosine receptor antagonist — it works by blocking the brain's fatigue signals. It also directly increases cortisol and adrenaline, elevates heart rate and blood pressure, and can precipitate panic attacks in people with panic disorder who are already running a sensitized autonomic nervous system. Caffeine intolerance is higher in people with anxiety. For many with panic disorder, eliminating or dramatically reducing caffeine produces noticeable reduction in baseline anxiety within days.

Sugar: Blood sugar spikes followed by reactive hypoglycemia create a physiological state — racing heart, shakiness, light-headedness, sweating — that is nearly indistinguishable from the early stages of a panic attack. For someone with panic disorder who is already hypervigilant about bodily sensations, a blood sugar crash can trigger a full panic episode. Stable blood sugar = fewer false alarms.

Alcohol: Creates temporary GABA enhancement (which is why it "calms" anxiety short-term) followed by a rebound effect that reduces GABA and increases glutamate excitability. The morning after drinking, the nervous system is in a more excitable state than baseline — a prime window for anxiety and panic. Alcohol also disrupts sleep architecture, reducing restorative slow-wave and REM sleep, which compounds anxiety the following day. Alcohol is uniquely bad for people with panic disorder.

Action Plan
Practical Protocol: Where to Start

You don't overhaul a diet overnight — especially when you're supporting someone else's household. Here's a protocol built for real life: sequential, sustainable, low-friction changes that compound into significant gut-brain improvement over 8–12 weeks.

1

Week 1–2: Remove the Biggest Disruptors

Start with subtraction, not addition. Eliminate or dramatically reduce: alcohol, added sugar, and ultra-processed snacks. Reduce caffeine gradually (abrupt caffeine withdrawal causes headaches and anxiety — taper over 2 weeks). This alone changes the gut environment measurably.

2

Week 2–3: Add One Fermented Food Daily

Plain yogurt with live cultures at breakfast. A tablespoon of kimchi or sauerkraut with lunch or dinner. Kefir as a snack. Pick one and make it habitual before adding more. Fermented foods introduce live bacteria and organic acids that begin shifting the microbiome within days.

3

Week 3–4: Increase Fiber Gradually

Add fiber incrementally — 5g more per week is the guideline. Rapid fiber increases cause gas and bloating, which can trigger anxiety in sensitive individuals. Add one serving of legumes, add berries to a meal, swap white bread for whole grain. The goal is 30–35g daily long-term.

4

Week 4–6: Omega-3s and Anti-Inflammatory Foods

Aim for 2–3 servings of fatty fish per week. Add walnuts as a daily snack. Use extra virgin olive oil as your primary cooking fat. These changes directly reduce systemic inflammation — which means less neuroinflammation, less amygdala hyperreactivity, less baseline anxiety.

5

Week 6–12: Blood Sugar Stability

Pair carbohydrates with protein and fat at every meal to blunt blood sugar spikes. Eat breakfast within an hour of waking. Don't skip meals. The goal is a steady blood glucose curve all day — eliminating the reactive hypoglycemia crashes that mimic and trigger panic.

6

Ongoing: Consider a Targeted Probiotic

If diet changes are in place and anxiety symptoms remain elevated, a high-quality probiotic with Lactobacillus rhamnosus and Bifidobacterium longum may provide additional benefit. Look for 10–50 billion CFU, multiple strains, refrigerated. Run for 8–12 weeks minimum before evaluating effect.

Remember: You can implement much of this in a shared household without announcing it as an "anxiety protocol." Better food is better food. The person you're supporting doesn't need to know you're also doing this for their gut-brain axis — and they probably don't need the pressure of knowing.

Signs the Gut-Brain Issue May Need Clinical Support

Dietary intervention is powerful but not a replacement for medical care in all situations. Consider consulting a doctor or registered dietitian if:

  • Significant GI symptoms are present (chronic diarrhea, constipation, bloating, abdominal pain) — these may indicate conditions like SIBO, IBS, or IBD that need diagnosis
  • Panic disorder is severe enough to be disabling — medication may need to be part of the picture, and dietary changes work alongside, not instead of, clinical treatment
  • Significant nutritional deficiencies are suspected (low energy, hair loss, poor wound healing) — blood work can identify specific gaps
  • The person has a history of disordered eating — dietary changes need to be navigated carefully in this context

A functional medicine doctor or integrative psychiatrist specifically is likely to be more conversant with the gut-brain axis research than a conventional primary care provider. Ask specifically about stool testing, gut permeability markers, and inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6) if you want a more complete picture of what's happening at the gut level.
